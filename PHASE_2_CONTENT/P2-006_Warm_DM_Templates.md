# P2-006: Warm DM Templates

## Context
Short, no-pitch variants for outbound DMs
Goal: Start conversation, offer perspective, no immediate ask

---

## TEMPLATE 1: The Consultative Open (General)

**When to use:** Cold DM to relevant connection, or responding to engagement on your posts

**Length:** 2 sentences

**Text:**

> Thanks for connecting. I help businesses like yours think through the technology and operations side of things — happy to chat if anything's on your radar.

**Variants:**

Variant A (Softer):
> Thanks for connecting. I usually end up talking to people about the gap between their systems and their actual operations — could be useful to compare notes sometime.

Variant B (More specific):
> Thanks for connecting. I help operations teams figure out what's actually broken before they pick tools. Would be useful to chat if you're evaluating anything soon.

Variant C (Reactive to their work):
> Thanks for connecting. Seeing a lot of interesting projects in [their industry]. Happy to share perspective on the operations side if you ever want to bounce ideas around.

---

## TEMPLATE 2: The Problem Recognition (Sector-Specific)

**When to use:** Connecting with someone in decorated goods, workwear, or print after seeing their role/activity

**Length:** 2–3 sentences

**Text:**

> Thanks for connecting. I spend a lot of time working with teams like yours on order management and approval workflows — usually find some quick wins without needing to overhaul everything. Would be worth a quick call if you're running into any of that.

**Variants:**

Variant A (Decorated goods focus):
> Thanks for connecting. I work with a lot of decorated goods operations on the approval bottleneck — the stuff that happens between design and production. Usually worth a conversation if you're managing that flow.

Variant B (Workwear focus):
> Thanks for connecting. I help workwear and apparel teams sort out their B2B portal and bulk ordering workflows. Seem like natural pain points in your space — happy to share what I'm seeing regularly.

Variant C (ERP focus):
> Thanks for connecting. I work with operations teams evaluating or replacing ERP systems. If you're thinking about that (or already in it), worth an honest conversation about what actually matters.

---

## TEMPLATE 3: The Insight Share (Post-Engagement)

**When to use:** Someone engaged meaningfully with your content (long comment, share, etc.)

**Length:** 2–3 sentences

**Text:**

> Thanks for the thoughtful comment on that post. Seems like you're dealing with similar stuff. Would be useful to connect — I'm always interested in what's actually happening on the ground vs. what the systems say is happening.

**Variants:**

Variant A (If they commented on ERP post):
> Thanks for weighing in on the ERP post — sounds like you've been through the wars on implementation. Would be worth comparing notes; I find the pattern is usually predictable if you know what to look for.

Variant B (If they commented on AI post):
> Thanks for the comment on AI. You're right that the tools matter less than the foundation. Would be good to chat about what you're seeing in your space.

Variant C (If they engaged with sector content):
> Thanks for the comment. Seems like you're in the thick of this stuff daily. Worth a conversation — I usually find there are 2–3 things that would unlock capacity without a major overhaul.

---

## TEMPLATE 4: The Referral Thank You + Offer (Post-Introduction)

**When to use:** Someone introduces you or passes your name to a contact

**Length:** 2–3 sentences

**Text:**

> Thanks for the intro. I'm not going to hard-sell [contact]'s team on anything, just honest about what I see and what usually works. But I appreciate the connection — always better when there's a trusted voice first.

**Variants:**

Variant A (Simpler):
> Thanks for the intro. Will be a good conversation.

Variant B (More detailed):
> Thanks for the intro to [name]. No pressure from me — just here to share perspective on what we usually find works in operations. Appreciate you thinking of them.

Variant C (Acknowledging context):
> Thanks for thinking of us for an intro. I know [name]'s team has been dealing with [issue], so should be a useful conversation. Appreciate you connecting the dots.

---

## TEMPLATE 5: The "I Noticed You" (Specific Observation)

**When to use:** You've done research and found something specific about their business

**Length:** 2–3 sentences

**Text:**

> I came across your work in [sector] and saw you're dealing with [specific situation]. That's exactly the kind of knot I usually help teams untangle. Would be worth a conversation if you're thinking about solutions.

**Variants:**

Variant A (Startup/growth company):
> I noticed you're scaling up fast in the decorated goods space. That's when the ops gaps usually show up most. Might be worth a conversation about what you're seeing as you grow.

Variant B (Recent product launch):
> Saw you just launched [portal/system]. That's a great move, but I'm curious how it's integrating with the rest of your workflow. Worth a quick call to compare notes.

Variant C (Award/recognition):
> Saw you won [award] — congrats. Usually when businesses are doing that well, there's an opportunity to unlock another 20% without much extra effort. Would be useful to chat.

---

## TEMPLATE 6: The Value-First Share (No Ask)

**When to use:** You have something specific to share (article, insight, relevant resource)

**Length:** 2–4 sentences

**Text:**

> Came across [article/resource/data] and thought of you — seems directly relevant to [their work]. No ask here, just thought it might be useful. Happy to chat if you want to dive into how this applies to your situation.

**Variants:**

Variant A (Sharing a resource):
> I wrote up some thoughts on [topic] that seemed relevant to what you're working on. Sharing in case it's useful. No pressure to read — just thought it might save you some time.

Variant B (Sharing data/insight):
> Been seeing a pattern in [sector] around [issue] — data suggests [finding]. Thought of your team because I suspect you're running into similar stuff. Would be interesting to hear if that matches what you're seeing.

Variant C (Sharing an article):
> Saw this article on [topic] and immediately thought it applied to [their specific situation]. Sharing because I think it's rare to see someone articulate [insight] that well. Curious if you're thinking about this too.

---

## TEMPLATE 7: The Ask (Honest & Low-Pressure)

**When to use:** After some rapport is established, when it makes sense to move toward a conversation

**Length:** 2–3 sentences

**Text:**

> You seem like someone who'd be good to have a real conversation with about [topic]. Not trying to sell you anything, just curious what you're seeing and whether a different perspective would be useful. Got 20 minutes for a call sometime soon?

**Variants:**

Variant A (More direct):
> Worth a conversation? I can usually find 2–3 quick wins in operations workflows. No commitment — just a solid 20-minute call about what you're dealing with.

Variant B (Tied to specific pain):
> You mentioned [specific issue] — that's exactly what I help teams fix. Would you be open to a quick conversation to see if there's something useful I could share?

Variant C (Soft ask):
> Would be worth 20 minutes to compare notes. I've worked with similar businesses and usually find some useful perspective. Let me know if that makes sense.

---

## Key Principles for All Templates

### DO:
- Keep it short (2–3 sentences max for DM)
- Lead with their context or need, not your expertise
- No sales language ("solution," "let me help you grow," "reach out")
- Sound like a human, not a template
- Offer perspective, not a pitch
- Make it easy to say yes (20 minutes, no commitment)

### DON'T:
- Ask for a favor upfront
- Lead with your credentials or accomplishments
- Write a paragraph — people won't read it
- Use "I help businesses like yours" without specificity
- Ask for a meeting in the first DM (wait until second exchange)
- Use emoji or excessive punctuation

---

## Response Patterns & Follow-Ups

### If They Don't Respond (After 5–7 Days)
Option A (Gentle reminder):
> Just circling back — would still be worth a conversation if timing works. No pressure.

Option B (Offer new value):
> Meant to send this earlier, but [resource/article/insight] seemed relevant to what you're doing. Thought of you again.

Option C (Acknowledge they're busy):
> Probably buried. Just wanted to make sure the invite landed. Happy to connect when things slow down.

### If They Engage But Don't Commit to a Call Yet
Option A (Deepen the conversation):
> That makes sense. [Respond to their comment]. Next question — are you evaluating [related thing], or still figuring out priorities?

Option B (Share relevant insight):
> Totally understand. A lot of teams are dealing with that right now. [Share 1–2 specific insights]. Worth a conversation if you want to explore options.

Option C (Acknowledge friction):
> Yeah, that's usually where it gets tricky. I run into that a lot. Might actually be worth 20 minutes to map out what's really happening. Interested?

### If They Say Yes
Option A (Confirm logistics):
> Great. How's your calendar? I'm flexible — whenever works for you. [Your timezone] good?

Option B (Set context):
> Perfect. Just to set expectations, I'll ask questions more than I'll talk. Genuinely curious about what you're dealing with. Let's find a time that works.

Option C (Move to email):
> Awesome. Let me send you a calendar link. [Link]. Any particular area you want to make sure we cover?

## Context-Specific Variations

### For Operations Managers
Use Templates 2, 5 (problem recognition, specific observation)
- Lead with operational pain
- Mention workflow, capacity, visibility
- Tie to their role (not CEO-level language)

### For Business Owners / C-Suite
Use Templates 1, 3, 7 (consultative, insight share, ask)
- Lead with business impact
- Mention revenue, growth, team efficiency
- Tie to strategy (not tactics)

### For IT / Systems Decision-Makers
Use Templates 2, 4, 6 (problem recognition, referral, value-first share)
- Lead with integration, systems alignment, technical architecture
- Mention data quality, system connectivity, user experience
- Tie to technical foundation

### For Warm Introductions
Use Templates 4, 7 (referral thank you, ask)
- Acknowledge the introducer
- Move quickly to call (trust is already there)
- Focus on context, not explanation

## Platform Notes

### LinkedIn DM
- Short form works (these templates are ideal)
- Can link to a post or article
- Emoji acceptable but minimal (1 max)
- Professional but conversational tone

### Email (If You Get Email Address)
- Expand slightly (add 1–2 more sentences of context)
- Lead with subject line: "[Name], [connection point]" or "[Name], quick perspective"
- Keep body to 3–4 sentences still
- Include soft CTA (20-minute call, not a meeting request)

### Phone/WhatsApp (If You Have Number)
- These templates work verbatim
- Voice call should follow up with text: "Meant to follow up from our call..."
- More casual but still professional

---

## Timing & Frequency

### Outbound DMs
- Send 3–5 per week (not all at once)
- Spread across 2–3 different days
- Mix templates 1, 2, 5 (general, sector-specific, observation)
- Wait 5–7 days before follow-up

### Post Engagement DMs
- Send within 24 hours of engagement
- Use Templates 3, 4 (insight share, intro thank you)
- Higher response rate (they already know you exist)

### Warm Introduction Follow-Ups
- Send within 2 days of intro
- Use Template 4, 7 (referral thank you, ask)
- More aggressive timeline (momentum matters)

---

## Tracking & Iteration

### Track
- Response rate by template
- Conversion rate by template (DM → call → deal)
- Engagement patterns (which roles respond best?)

### Test
- Shorter vs. longer templates (currently all short)
- Different openings (value-first vs. consultative)
- Sector-specific vs. generic
- Timing (Monday vs. Wednesday vs. Thursday)

### Optimize
- Rotate templates monthly (don't use the same one repetitively)
- Double down on templates with 30%+ response rate
- Retire templates with <10% response rate
- A/B test openings (which first line generates most replies?)
