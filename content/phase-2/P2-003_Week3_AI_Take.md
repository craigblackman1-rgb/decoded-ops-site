## VARIANT 1: Direct Framing (Conversational)

**Headline:**
I'm Not Here to Sell You an AI Tool

**Body:**

Here's what I actually tell clients when they ask about AI:

"I'm not here to sell you an AI tool. I'm here to tell you honestly whether AI will help your business right now, what you need to fix first, and where the real opportunities are in your specific operation."

Most AI conversations start in the wrong place.

They start with the tool: ChatGPT, Claude, automation platforms, whatever the latest thing is. But the tool doesn't matter if:
- Your data isn't clean
- Your processes are still manual and chaotic
- Your team doesn't know how to work together yet
- You're still copying data between three different systems

I've seen businesses spend £50k on AI automation only to realize their problem wasn't AI. It was that nobody had a clear picture of their actual workflow.

**Here's what I actually look for:**

1. **What's the real bottleneck?** (Is it speed? Accuracy? Visibility? Cost?)
2. **Can you describe it in detail?** (If you can't define the problem, AI won't solve it)
3. **What needs to happen first?** (Usually: clean data, clear process, visibility)
4. **Where does AI actually move the needle?** (Not everywhere. Somewhere specific.)
5. **What's the real ROI?** (Time saved? Accuracy improved? Cost down? Revenue up?)

Most AI opportunities live in step 3–5, not in "let's buy the newest AI tool."

**The honest truth:**

AI is useful. Really useful. But only after you've fixed the foundations.

If your operations are messy, AI makes messy *faster*.

If your operations are clear, AI makes them smarter.

That's the conversation I have with people.

**CTA:**
If you're thinking about AI and you want to know whether it's right for your business (and what to do first), happy to talk through it.

## VARIANT 2: Story-Driven (More Narrative)

**Headline:**
What I Actually Tell Clients About AI

**Body:**

When someone asks me about AI for their business, here's what I say:

"I'm not here to sell you an AI tool. I'm here to tell you honestly whether AI will help your business right now, what you need to fix first, and where the real opportunities are in your specific operation."

It's not the answer they expect. Usually they're hoping I'll say, "Yes, you need Claude/ChatGPT/automation — here's how to implement it."

But I've watched businesses do that. Buy the shiny tool. Spend 3–6 months setting it up. Realize it didn't actually solve the problem because the problem wasn't "we need AI," it was "our processes are a mess."

One business spent £50k on workflow automation. Turned out their real issue was that they didn't have a clear picture of their own operations. The automation made bad processes fast.

**Here's what actually works:**

Before I recommend an AI tool, we answer:

1. What are we trying to fix? (Speed? Accuracy? Visibility? Cost? Revenue?)
2. Can we describe it precisely? (Vague problems don't have AI solutions)
3. What has to happen first? (Usually: data quality, process clarity, visibility)
4. Where specifically does AI help? (Not everywhere. Somewhere specific.)
5. What does success actually look like? (Can we measure it?)

Most of the time, the real opportunity is in steps 3–4. The AI piece comes *after* you've done the foundational work.

**The honest truth:**

AI is powerful. But it's not magic, and it's not always the answer.

If your operations are disorganized, AI accelerates the chaos.

If your operations are organized, AI makes them smarter and faster.

I'm much more interested in the second scenario.

## VARIANT 3: Principle-Based (Educational)

**Headline:**
The One Question I Ask Before Recommending AI

**Body:**

I have one rule for recommending AI to a business:

"I'm not here to sell you an AI tool. I'm here to tell you honestly whether AI will help your business right now, what you need to fix first, and where the real opportunities are in your specific operation."

That rule comes from watching too many AI projects fail.

Fail not because the AI was bad, but because the business picked the wrong problem to solve.

**The pattern I see:**

A business hears about AI. They get excited. They decide they need "AI automation" or "AI forecasting" or "AI customer service."

They buy a tool. They spend money. They spend time.

Then they realize: the AI can't help because their data is messy, or their process is broken, or they don't have visibility into what they're trying to fix.

I worked with a business that spent £50k on automation. The real problem wasn't "we need to automate this task." It was "we don't actually know what our workflow looks like."

Automation made the problem worse, not better.

**Here's what I do instead:**

Before touching any AI tool, I ask:

1. What's the actual bottleneck? (Data? Speed? Accuracy? Decision-making?)
2. Can you describe it in detail? (Vague = no AI solution)
3. What foundations do you need first? (Data quality? Process clarity? Visibility?)
4. Where does AI create real value? (Somewhere specific, not everywhere)
5. How do we measure success? (Can we quantify the impact?)

Most of the time, the real opportunity isn't "buy an AI tool." It's "fix the foundations first, then AI will help."

**The opportunity:**

AI is genuinely useful — but only after you've done the work to know what you're trying to fix and why.

**CTA:**
If you're thinking about AI and you want a realistic assessment of whether it'll actually help your business, I'm happy to talk through it.
